## SECTION 1: THE PLATFORM DECISION

*Why this is the most important decision you'll make — and why most businesses get it catastrophically wrong.*

---

Most businesses end up on the wrong platform because they chose it for the wrong reason. "My competitor is on Google so I should be on Google." "I saw an agency on Instagram so I'll use Meta." "My nephew said TikTok Ads are cheap right now."

None of these are the right reason.

Platform selection is the single highest-leverage decision in paid acquisition. Get it wrong and you can spend six months and £10,000 building an audience that never converts. Get it right and you generate leads from week one.

Here are the four questions that determine your platform.

---

### The Four Decision Questions

**Question 1: Is there active demand for what you sell?**

Active demand means people are already searching for your service. They know the problem they have. They know the solution exists. They are typing it into Google right now.

"Emergency plumber London." "Dental implants Manchester." "Family solicitor Birmingham." These are high-intent searches. Someone typing these words is not browsing — they are in buying mode.

If active demand exists at volume: Google Ads is your primary channel.

If active demand is thin, scattered, or doesn't exist yet: Meta Ads is your primary channel, because you are creating demand rather than capturing it.

**Question 2: What is your average ticket size?**

This matters because it determines your tolerance for cost-per-click.

High-ticket services (dental implants £2,000+, personal injury legal cases, business IT support contracts) can afford expensive clicks. A dental implant keyword might cost £8-£15 per click. If you convert 8% of landing page visitors into consultations, and 60% of consultations convert into patients, your cost per patient is roughly £100-£200. For a £2,500 implant, that's a phenomenal return.

Low-ticket services (cleaning, gardening, handyman, mobile car wash) cannot absorb expensive clicks. A cleaning enquiry is worth £30-£80 per month to a business — maybe £500 over a year. Paying £15 per click for a service worth £500 total puts you under extreme pressure. You need either high conversion rates or low CPCs — usually only achievable on Meta.

The rule: if your average ticket is under £300, start on Meta. Over £300, Google is viable. Over £1,000, Google should be primary.

**Question 3: How fast does the buyer decide?**

Emergency services (plumbing, locksmith, electricians, emergency dental) have buyers who decide in minutes. They search, they call, they book. Google Search captures them at the exact moment of need.

Considered purchases (cosmetic dentistry, solicitors for property transactions, business insurance) have buyers who research over days or weeks. They need to see you multiple times. Meta Ads retargeting is essential here — Google gets them into the funnel, Meta nurtures them to conversion.

Complex B2B services have the longest cycles. These often need LinkedIn Ads in the mix, or heavy email nurture off Meta Ads traffic.

**Question 4: What is your monthly budget?**

This is uncomfortable but necessary.

Google Ads in competitive local markets requires a minimum viable budget to generate enough data for the algorithm to optimise. As a rule of thumb: £500-£800 per month is the floor for most local service Google campaigns. Below that, you won't generate enough clicks and conversions for smart bidding to function.

Meta Ads can work from £300 per month for a single local service area, though £500+ is where you get real learning.

If your total budget is under £500/month: Meta Ads first, master it, then add Google when you scale.

If your budget is £500-£1,500/month: choose one platform and go deep rather than splitting thin.

If your budget is £1,500+/month: dual-channel is viable and recommended for most service businesses.

---

### Platform Decision Tree by Business Type

**Dental Practice — Google First, Then Meta**

Dentists have high-ticket services (implants, Invisalign, composite bonding) with active search demand. Someone searching "Invisalign cost Birmingham" is already sold on Invisalign — they are comparing providers. Capture them at the moment of intent with Google. Add Meta for retargeting website visitors and running awareness campaigns for treatments people don't know they want yet (teeth whitening, smile makeovers).

Budget split (if dual): 65% Google, 35% Meta.

**Cleaning Company — Meta First**

Cleaning is a low-ticket service. Average monthly revenue per client: £60-£200. Google CPCs for "end of tenancy cleaning London" can hit £4-£8. You need very high conversion rates to make those economics work. Meta Ads let you target by location, homeowner status, and behavioural signals at much lower CPCs (£0.40-£1.20 typically). You can also use creative to make your service visually compelling — before/after content, process videos — which isn't possible with text ads.

Budget: Meta-only until £1,500/month total budget.

**Legal Services — Both Platforms**

Law has both high active demand (people searching for specific legal help) and complex consideration cycles. Personal injury, conveyancing, family law, immigration — all have strong search intent. But legal decisions are also high-stakes and slow. Retargeting is essential. Someone who visited your conveyancing page but didn't call needs to see your firm three more times before they pick up the phone.

Google captures the intent. Meta handles the follow-up.

Budget split: 55% Google, 45% Meta.

**Trades (Plumber, Electrician, Builder, Roofer) — Both, With Google Primary**

Trades split into two categories: emergency and planned.

Emergency (boiler repair, burst pipe, electrical fault) — pure Google. These buyers search and call within minutes. They do not browse Facebook for a plumber when their kitchen is flooding.

Planned (loft conversion, new bathroom, rewiring a house) — both platforms. Google captures people actively planning. Meta reaches homeowners who haven't started planning yet but fit the profile (new homeowners, people in certain postcodes, homeowners who've engaged with home improvement content).

Budget split for mixed trades business: 60% Google, 40% Meta.

**Wellness and Beauty (Aesthetics, Physio, Personal Training) — Meta First**

These services often require visual proof of results. Before/after photos, practitioner videos, transformation stories. This creative-first approach suits Meta Ads architecture. The visual feed format lets you show results. Google is secondary for capturing people already searching.

Exception: high-ticket aesthetic treatments (lip filler, anti-wrinkle, CoolSculpting) have growing search demand and should have a Google presence too.

We have had clients come to us having spent £6,000 on Google Ads for a cleaning company over four months. Their CPC was £5.20. Their conversion rate was 2.1%. Their cost per lead was £247. Platform selection is not a preference. It is maths.

## SECTION 2: CAMPAIGN ARCHITECTURE — THE STRUCTURE THAT WORKS

*Most businesses are handed a campaign. They should be handed a system.*

---

Campaign architecture is not about clicking buttons in an ad platform. It is about designing a logical, scalable structure that generates clean data, enables intelligent optimisation, and gives you full control over where your money goes and why.

Here is how to think about it.

---

### The Hierarchy: Campaign → Ad Set → Ad

Every ad platform uses a three-level hierarchy. Understanding it is not optional.

**The Campaign Level** is where you set your objective. What do you want the platform to optimise for? Conversions (phone calls, form submissions, bookings)? Traffic (clicks to your website)? Leads (native lead forms)?

The objective at campaign level tells the algorithm what it is trying to achieve. If you set a traffic objective and then complain your ads aren't driving phone calls, that is not the algorithm's fault. You told it to drive clicks. It drove clicks.

Always choose Conversions as your campaign objective for service businesses. Not traffic. Not reach. Conversions. Your tracking must be set up before you do this (see Section 3).

**The Ad Set Level** is where you set your targeting, budget, schedule, placements, and bidding strategy. Each ad set represents a distinct audience, keyword group, or targeting angle.

A common mistake: lumping all keywords or all audiences into a single ad set. This destroys your ability to see what is and isn't working. If one keyword is generating leads at £8 and another at £80, you need to be able to see that and act on it. You can't if they're in the same ad set.

**The Ad Level** is where your creative lives: headlines, descriptions, images, videos, copy. Multiple ads per ad set allows A/B testing. Always have at least 2, ideally 3-4 ads per ad set, so the platform can find the winner.

---

### Google Ads: The Full Architecture

**Keyword Research Methodology**

Do not start with keyword tools. Start with the customer.

Ask: what words does a person in pain type into Google? Not the clinical industry term — the human, frustrated-at-2am search term.

"Dentist near me" — yes.
"Dental implant cost" — yes.
"Odontological restoration service" — no human has ever typed that.

Build your keyword list from three sources:

1. **Pain-based terms**: "my tooth hurts", "chipped tooth fix", "gap in teeth"
2. **Solution-based terms**: "dental implants", "veneers", "Invisalign"
3. **Competitor/category terms**: "private dentist [city]", "NHS dentist accepting patients"

Then run those through Google Keyword Planner to find actual volume, CPCs, and related terms you missed.

**Match Types — Use Them Correctly**

Three match types. Each has a role.

Exact Match [keyword] — your ad shows only when someone searches that specific phrase (with close variants). Use for your highest-intent, highest-converting keywords. These should get the most budget.

Phrase Match "keyword" — your ad shows when someone searches a phrase containing your keyword. More reach than exact, more control than broad. Use for your secondary keyword list.

Broad Match keyword — your ad can show for any related search. Powerful when paired with smart bidding and conversion data, dangerous when you're starting out. Do not use broad match on a new campaign without at least 30 conversions in the account.

**Negative Keywords — The Most Underused Tool in Paid Search**

Negative keywords tell Google when NOT to show your ad. They are how you stop wasting money on irrelevant clicks.

For every service business, build a negative keyword list before you launch. Include:

- "free" (unless you offer a free consultation, in which case add it to your positive list)
- "DIY", "how to", "course", "training", "jobs", "salary", "career"
- Competitor names (unless you're deliberately bidding on competitors)
- Geographic terms outside your service area
- Related but irrelevant services (a cosmetic dentist should negative "NHS")

Review your search terms report weekly for the first month. Add new negatives every week. A mature campaign should have 50-200 negative keywords.

**Bidding Strategy Progression**

Do not start on Target CPA. The algorithm needs data to hit a target.

Stage 1 (Weeks 1-4): Maximize Clicks with a manual CPC bid cap. This generates traffic and data while keeping costs controlled. Set a bid cap based on your economics — if a lead is worth £50 to you and you expect a 5% conversion rate, your maximum CPC should be around £2.50.

Stage 2 (Month 2, once you have 20+ conversions): Switch to Maximize Conversions. The algorithm now optimises for conversion events rather than just clicks.

Stage 3 (Month 3, once you have 30-50 conversions): Set a Target CPA. Tell Google what you want to pay per conversion. It will optimise toward that target. This is where campaigns become efficient at scale.

Jumping straight to Target CPA with no conversion data results in the algorithm making random decisions. Give it the data it needs first.

**Quality Score — Why It Determines Your CPC**

Quality Score (1-10) is Google's rating of the relevance between your keyword, your ad, and your landing page. A high Quality Score means you pay less per click for the same position.

A keyword with Quality Score 8 pays roughly 50% less per click than the same keyword with Quality Score 4.

Improve Quality Score by:
- Matching your ad copy closely to the keyword (if someone searches "emergency dentist", your headline should say "Emergency Dentist")
- Matching your landing page content to the keyword and ad
- Improving your click-through rate (better CTR signals relevance to Google)

**Campaign Structure for Local Service Business**

Campaign 1: Core Services — exact and phrase match on your primary services
Campaign 2: Location + Service — "[city] + [service]" keywords
Campaign 3: Emergency/Urgent — high-intent emergency searches (if applicable)
Campaign 4: Competitor keywords (optional, run separately to contain spend)

Each campaign has 2-4 ad sets, each targeting a distinct keyword theme. Each ad set has 3-4 ad variations.

---

### Meta Ads: The Full Architecture

**Broad vs Narrow Targeting**

This is where most Meta Ads accounts go wrong.

In 2020, the conventional wisdom was to layer interest targeting tightly: homeowners, aged 35-55, interested in home improvement, who have engaged with competitors. The algorithm needed this guidance because it had less data.

In 2025, Meta's algorithm is significantly more capable. Tight interest stacking often limits delivery and raises CPMs without improving lead quality. The algorithm needs room to find your buyers.

For local service businesses: broad targeting with tight geography is usually the winning approach.

Define your audience by:
1. Location (your service radius — be specific, 10-mile or specific postcodes)
2. Age range (minimum viable range — don't over-restrict)
3. Exclude existing customers (upload your customer list)

Let Meta find the buyers within that geography. Trust the algorithm, measure the results.

**When Narrow Targeting Still Works**

Narrow targeting (layered interests) remains useful for:

1. New accounts with no pixel data (the algorithm needs some guidance before it has enough data to optimise)
2. Very niche services with a specific demographic (e.g., a high-end aesthetic clinic targeting women 30-55 with household income signals)
3. Retargeting campaigns (your website visitors, video viewers, lead form openers — these are the tightest, highest-converting audiences)

**Budget Per Ad Set Rule**

Each ad set needs enough budget to generate at least 50 conversion events per week for the algorithm to exit the learning phase and optimise properly.

For local service businesses with an average CPA of £20-£60: you need roughly £100-£300 per week per ad set in the learning phase, then you can pull back once the algorithm has learned.

Do not run five ad sets at £10/day each and wonder why nothing is working. Concentrate budget.

**Frequency Rules**

Frequency = the average number of times each person in your audience has seen your ad.

For prospecting (cold audience): frequency above 4-5 per week means you're saturating. Refresh creative or expand audience.

For retargeting (warm audience): frequency of 7-10 over a 30-day window is fine — you want them to see you repeatedly.

Check frequency weekly. Declining CTR + rising frequency = creative fatigue. Time for new ads.

---

### The 3 Elements of a Converting Ad

Whether it's a Google text ad or a Meta video, every converting ad has three components.

**1. Pattern Interrupt**

The job of the first second (Meta) or first headline (Google) is to stop the scroll or earn the click. It must be specific and surprising. Vague is invisible.

Bad: "Professional Dental Care in Manchester"
Good: "Most Manchester Patients Wait 3 Weeks For An Implant Consultation. We See You This Week."

Bad: "Reliable Cleaning Services"
Good: "We Cleaned 47 Homes In Salford Last Month. Here's What They Said."

Specificity creates pattern interrupts. Generalities don't.

**2. Value Proposition**

After the pattern interrupt earns attention, you have 3-8 seconds (Meta) or 2 headlines (Google) to communicate the core offer. What do they get? Why should they choose you? What is the specific outcome?

This is not about your business. It is about their life after they hire you.

Bad: "We offer a full range of dental treatments."
Good: "Straight teeth in 6 months. No metal. No self-consciousness. We're the top-rated Invisalign provider in the city."

**3. Clear Call to Action**

Tell them exactly what to do next. Remove ambiguity. Friction kills conversions.

Not: "Get in touch."
Yes: "Book your free consultation today — spaces fill within 48 hours."

Not: "Learn more."
Yes: "See our full pricing and book online."

The CTA should match where the buyer is in their journey. Cold audience: "Book a free call." Warm retargeting audience: "You visited our site last week — we have a space for you this Friday. Book now."

---

### What Campaign Architecture Actually Means

Most businesses think campaign architecture means making a campaign and putting some ads in it. It doesn't.

Architecture means designing a system where:

- Every pound of budget is allocated to a specific purpose
- You can see exactly which keyword, audience, or creative is generating leads
- You can scale what works and kill what doesn't
- The algorithm has clean, uncontaminated data to optimise from
- You can walk away for a long weekend and know the data you come back to is meaningful

A badly structured campaign generates noise. You spend money, you get some leads, you can't tell why, and you can't improve it systematically. A well-structured campaign generates signal. Every metric means something.

## SECTION 3: CONVERSION TRACKING — THE STEP 90% OF BUSINESSES SKIP

*You cannot optimise what you cannot measure. You cannot measure what you haven't tracked.*

---

This section will feel technical. Push through it. Conversion tracking is the single most important piece of infrastructure in your entire paid acquisition setup. Without it, everything else is opinion.

---

### What Tracking Actually Is

When someone clicks your ad, visits your website, and fills in your contact form, your ad platform needs to know that happened. By default, it does not know. It knows someone clicked. It does not know what happened after the click.

Tracking is the process of linking what happens on your website (form submissions, phone calls, bookings, purchases) back to the specific ad that drove that behaviour.

Without tracking, you are paying for clicks into a black hole. You know you spent money. You might know you got some leads. You do not know which ad, which keyword, which audience drove which leads. You cannot optimise anything.

With proper tracking, you know:
- Which keywords have a £15 cost-per-lead and which have a £180 cost-per-lead
- Which ad creative drives qualified enquiries vs tire-kickers
- Which audience on Meta converts at twice the rate of others
- Your actual return on ad spend, to the penny

This is the difference between running ads and running a business.

---

### The 5 Conversion Events Every Business Must Track

**1. Form Submission (Contact/Enquiry Form)**
Someone fills in your website contact form. This is the most common lead event. Set this up as a conversion on every platform you're advertising on.

**2. Phone Call Click (Click-to-Call)**
On mobile, people click phone numbers rather than filling forms. If you have a phone number on your site and don't track clicks on it, you're missing a large percentage of your leads. Use Google Tag Manager to track phone number click events.

**3. Booking Confirmed (Calendly/Booking Tool)**
If you use Calendly, Acuity, or any booking tool, you need to track confirmed bookings as a conversion event — not just the booking page visit. The confirmation page URL or the Calendly webhook event is your trigger.

**4. Thank You Page View**
After any form submission or action, redirect to a dedicated /thank-you page. Track this page view as a conversion. This is the simplest, most reliable conversion tracking method. Don't use generic page views — only the specific /thank-you URL.

**5. WhatsApp / Live Chat Initiation**
If you use WhatsApp Business or live chat, track the first click or initiation. These are leads. They should be counted.

---

### The Technical Setup: Step by Step

**Step 1: Install Google Tag Manager (GTM)**

GTM is a container that lets you deploy tracking codes without touching your website code every time. Install it once, manage everything from the GTM interface.

1. Create a GTM account at tagmanager.google.com
2. Create a Container for your website
3. Add the GTM snippet to your website — one snippet in the `<head>`, one in the `<body>`
4. Verify installation with the GTM Preview mode

**Step 2: Set Up GA4**

1. Create a GA4 property in Google Analytics
2. In GA4, go to Data Streams → Add Web Stream → enter your URL
3. Copy your Measurement ID (G-XXXXXXXXXX)
4. In GTM, create a new Tag → Google Analytics: GA4 Configuration → paste your Measurement ID
5. Set the trigger to All Pages
6. Publish

**Step 3: Configure GA4 Conversion Events**

1. In GTM, create a new Tag → Google Analytics: GA4 Event
2. For form submissions: trigger on the /thank-you page view, event name "generate_lead"
3. For phone clicks: trigger on click of phone number elements, event name "phone_call"
4. In GA4, go to Configure → Events → find these events → toggle "Mark as conversion"

**Step 4: Link GA4 to Google Ads**

1. In Google Ads: Tools & Settings → Linked Accounts → Google Analytics
2. Link your GA4 property
3. Import your GA4 conversion events into Google Ads
4. Set these imported conversions as your "primary conversions" that smart bidding optimises toward

**Step 5: Install the Meta Pixel**

1. In Meta Business Manager: Events Manager → Connect Data Sources → Web → Meta Pixel
2. In GTM, create a new Tag → Custom HTML → paste the Meta Pixel base code
3. Set trigger to All Pages
4. Create a second Tag for the Lead event: trigger on /thank-you page, add the fbq('track', 'Lead') event
5. Verify with Meta Pixel Helper Chrome extension

**Step 6: UTM Parameters**

UTMs are URL parameters that identify where traffic came from. Always append them to your ad destination URLs.

Standard UTM structure:
```
https://yoursite.com/landing-page?utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&utm_campaign=campaign-name&utm_content=ad-variation-name
```

For Meta Ads, Meta auto-populates `{{campaign.name}}`, `{{adset.name}}`, `{{ad.name}}` dynamically — use these in your URL parameters template in Meta Ads Manager.

For Google Ads, enable Auto-Tagging (it's on by default) — this automatically appends Google's GCLID parameter, which GA4 uses to attribute sessions to specific keywords and ads.

### The One Configuration Mistake That Makes All Your Data Wrong

**The conversion window.**

Your conversion window tells the platform: "count a conversion if someone clicked my ad and then converted within X days."

The default Google Ads conversion window is 30 days. The default Meta Ads attribution window is 7-day click, 1-day view.

The mistake: leaving these at default without thinking about your sales cycle.

If your average lead-to-appointment time is 3 days: a 30-day conversion window is fine.

If you're a conveyancing solicitor with a 90-day transaction cycle: you might need to extend the window to capture conversions that happen weeks after the initial ad click.

If you're a B2B service: consider a 60-day or 90-day window.

More critically: **do not attribute a single conversion to multiple campaigns**. If Google Ads and Meta Ads both claim credit for the same lead (the person clicked a Google ad on Monday and a Meta ad on Friday before converting), you will double-count conversions. Your CPA will look lower than it is.

Solution: in Google Ads, set your attribution model to "Data-driven" if you have enough conversion volume, or "Last click" if you don't. In Meta, use "7-day click" attribution and accept that there will be some overlap — but track your actual leads separately (in a CRM or even a spreadsheet) to validate what the platforms are telling you.

---

### How to Verify Your Tracking Is Actually Working

Setting up tracking is not the same as having working tracking. This is a critical distinction.

**For Google Ads:**

1. Click one of your own ads (use a VPN or Incognito + different IP to avoid inflating your cost)
2. Complete the conversion action on your site (submit the form, reach the thank-you page)
3. In Google Ads: Tools → Conversions → check that the conversion shows as "Recent conversions"
4. Also verify in GA4: Realtime report → you should see a "generate_lead" event

**For Meta Ads:**

1. Install Meta Pixel Helper Chrome extension
2. Visit your landing page — Pixel Helper should show "PageView"
3. Complete your form/reach thank-you page — Pixel Helper should show "Lead" event
4. In Meta Events Manager: check that the Lead event shows "Active" status with recent test activity

**Do this before you spend a single penny on ads. Tracking that seems like it's working but isn't will silently corrupt your data for months.**

---

### What to Do When Tracking Breaks Mid-Campaign

Tracking breaks. Websites get updated, plugins conflict, developers accidentally remove tag containers. It happens.

Signs your tracking has broken:
- Conversion volume drops to zero suddenly (not gradually)
- CPA suddenly doubles or triples with no creative/targeting changes
- GA4 shows a significant traffic drop that doesn't match anything in your business
- Meta Events Manager shows your Pixel as "Inactive"

Immediate steps:
1. Check GTM Preview mode — is the container firing correctly?
2. Check the specific tags for your conversion events — are they triggering?
3. Check your landing page source code — is the GTM container snippet still there?
4. If you recently changed your website or CMS: check if the GTM snippet was preserved

If tracking breaks for more than 48 hours on an active campaign: pause the campaign's smart bidding and revert to manual CPC while you fix it. Smart bidding without conversion data will make increasingly bad decisions.

Fix the tracking, verify it's working again, then resume and allow 3-7 days for the algorithm to re-stabilise.

## SECTION 4: THE 48-HOUR CAMPAIGN HEALTH CHECK

*Most campaigns that fail were already showing warning signs at hour 24. Here's how to read them.*

---

The first 48 hours of a new campaign are not about results. They are about signals. The data you see in the first two days tells you whether the setup is sound, whether the algorithm is learning correctly, and whether you need to intervene before you waste more budget.

Here is exactly what to look at and what it means.

---

### Day 1 Metrics Check (First 24 Hours)

**Google Ads — What to Check**

Check: Impression Share
What's normal: 30-60% impression share on day 1 for a new campaign with reasonable bids.
Warning sign: below 10% impression share. Your bids are likely too low or your Quality Score is dragging your Ad Rank down. Check your Auction Insights and compare your CPCs to the suggested bid ranges.

Check: CTR (Click-Through Rate)
UK benchmark for local service Google Search: 4-8% for branded/high-intent terms, 2-4% for general service terms.
Warning sign: below 1.5% CTR. Your ad copy is not matching search intent. Review your top keywords and ensure your headlines directly mirror the search terms. Check if you have responsive search ad (RSA) assets that are highly relevant.

Check: Average CPC
Benchmark: highly variable by industry, but for context — dental: £2-£8, legal: £3-£15, trades: £1-£4, cleaning: £0.80-£3.
Warning sign: CPC is more than 2x your expected benchmark. Either your keywords are more competitive than modelled, or your Quality Score is low (increasing your CPC). Run a search terms report — are you showing for high-competition generic terms you didn't intend?

Check: Search Terms Report
What to look for: are the actual searches triggering your ads relevant to your business?
Warning sign: you're showing for irrelevant terms. Add negatives immediately. Every irrelevant click is budget wasted. This is especially common with phrase match keywords in the early days.

**Meta Ads — What to Check**

Check: CPM (Cost Per 1,000 Impressions)
UK benchmark for local service targeting: £8-£25 CPM depending on audience size and competition.
Warning sign: CPM above £40 with a small, tight audience. Your audience is too narrow. Loosen targeting or expand geography slightly.
Warning sign: CPM below £3 for a professional service audience. Check your targeting — you may be reaching a very broad, low-value audience.

Check: Link CTR (percentage of people who click the link in your ad)
Benchmark: 1.5-3% is healthy for cold audience ads. Above 3% is excellent. Below 0.8% is concerning.
Warning sign: below 0.8% on day 1. Your creative is not compelling enough to earn a click. The pattern interrupt is failing. This needs a creative change, not a targeting change.

Check: Ad Delivery Status
What to look for: is your ad set out of the Learning Phase?
Normal: you will still be in Learning Phase after 24 hours. This is expected and fine.
Warning sign: "Learning Limited" status. This means the ad set is generating fewer than 50 optimisation events per week. Either your budget is too low, your audience is too small, or your CTA event is too rare (e.g., optimising for phone calls that happen twice a week). Consider broadening your audience or lowering budget concentration.

---

### Day 2 Metrics Check (Hour 25-48)

By day 2 you should have enough data to see directional trends. Not definitive conclusions — directional trends.

**The 3 Signals a Campaign Is Working**

Signal 1: CTR is at or above benchmark and stable or improving.
This means your creative is resonating. The audience recognises the problem you're describing and is engaging.

Signal 2: Search terms (Google) or audience reach (Meta) are showing high relevance.
On Google: your search terms report is showing searches that genuinely reflect buying intent — people who have the problem your service solves.
On Meta: your reach is building at an efficient CPM, and the frequency is below 2 (you're reaching new people, not burning your audience).

Signal 3: Landing page traffic is converting.
Even without confirmed leads yet, you can see in GA4 whether people are spending time on your landing page, scrolling, and engaging with your CTA. A bounce rate above 80% on paid traffic within the first 48 hours is an early warning sign that the message-to-page match is weak.

**The 3 Signals a Campaign Is Dying**

Signal 1: CTR collapses after day 1.
If your CTR was 3% on day 1 and 1.2% on day 2, something has changed. Check: did Meta shift your ad delivery to a different placement? Did Google start triggering your ad for less relevant queries? Did your bid get outcompeted and you're now appearing in a lower position?

Signal 2: Spend is not being used.
Budget underspend of more than 30% on day 2 means the algorithm is struggling to find eligible impressions. Check: is your target audience large enough? Are your bids competitive? Is your ad approved and active?

Signal 3: Impressions but zero clicks.
If you're getting thousands of impressions but no clicks, your ad is appearing but failing to earn attention. On Google: your headline is not matching intent. On Meta: your visual hook is not stopping the scroll. This requires creative intervention, not a wait-and-see approach.

### Common Day-1 Errors That Kill Campaigns

These are not strategy errors. These are setup errors that manifest in the first 24 hours.

**Error 1: Destination URL mismatch**
The ad is sending traffic to your homepage, not a dedicated landing page. Google penalises relevance mismatches (hurts Quality Score, raises CPC). Meta sends traffic to a generic page that doesn't convert. Check: click your own ad and verify where you land.

**Error 2: Conversion tracking not firing**
The most painful day-1 error. You're spending money, getting clicks, potentially getting leads — but nothing is being tracked. Smart bidding has no signal to optimise toward. Check your tracking before launch, every single time.

**Error 3: Ads running at wrong hours**
If you're a local service business that only operates 8am-6pm, running ads at 2am generates clicks from people you can't serve. Check your ad schedule — most local service businesses should restrict hours to their operating window plus a few hours either side.

**Error 4: Geographic targeting too broad or wrong**
Running a local plumber's campaign nationally (even if accidentally set to "UK" instead of the specific city). On Google, verify your location targeting. On Meta, double-check your radius is centred on the correct postcode and is the right radius.

**Error 5: Budget set at campaign level instead of ad set level (Meta)**
On Meta, if you set a campaign budget (CBO — Campaign Budget Optimisation), Meta decides how to distribute budget across ad sets. Sometimes it funnels everything into one ad set and starves the others. For early testing, use ad set level budgets (ABO) so you control exactly how much each ad set gets.

## SECTION 5: THE AGENCY AUDIT — 10 QUESTIONS EVERY BUSINESS OWNER MUST ASK

*You are about to give someone £1,000+ per month of your money. Here's how to know if they deserve it.*

---

The agency market for paid advertising is flooded. It is one of the easiest businesses to start: get a Facebook account, call yourself a media buyer, charge £500/month, deliver vague reports. Many people are doing exactly this.

Some are genuinely excellent. Some are fraudulent. Most are mediocre.

Here are the 10 questions that separate the real from the fake. Ask them before you sign anything.

---

### The 10 Questions

**Question 1: "Show me an example of a campaign you've run in my industry. What were the specific results?"**

Why it matters: Generic case studies are worthless. "We generated leads for a client" tells you nothing. An agency with real results will have specific numbers: platform, industry, spend, CPL, conversion rate, revenue generated. They should be able to show you a screenshot of the actual account dashboard.

Good answer: "We managed a Google Ads campaign for a dental practice in Leeds. Over 6 months we generated 340 new patient enquiries at an average CPL of £38. 62% of those booked consultations. I can show you the account performance screenshot."

Bad answer: "We've worked with many businesses in the health sector and have a strong track record of driving results." (Notice: no numbers. No platform. No specifics.)

**Question 2: "Who specifically will be managing my account? What is their experience level?"**

Why it matters: Many agencies win business with a senior salesperson, then hand the account to a 22-year-old who passed a Google Ads exam two months ago. You are paying for expertise. Know whose expertise you're actually buying.

Good answer: "Your account will be managed by [specific name], who has been running paid media for 4 years with specific experience in [your industry]. You'll have direct Slack/WhatsApp access to them, not just the account manager."

Bad answer: "Our team of experts will manage your campaign across all departments." (Vague. Collective noun. No specific person.)

**Question 3: "How do you measure success? What specific KPIs will you report on and at what frequency?"**

Why it matters: An agency that cannot tell you their success metrics before starting cannot be held accountable for results. You need to agree on what "working" looks like before you spend money.

Good answer: "We'll report on CPL, conversion rate, cost per booked appointment, and return on ad spend. You get a live dashboard you can check anytime, plus a weekly summary and a monthly strategy call. If CPL exceeds £X, we discuss changes proactively."

Bad answer: "We'll send you monthly reports." (What's in them? How do they decide what to optimise? What happens if things aren't working?)

**Question 4: "What is your minimum contract term and what are the exit terms?"**

Why it matters: A 12-month minimum contract for a new agency relationship is a red flag. Reputable agencies don't need to lock you in for a year. If the results are good, you'll stay. If they're not, you should be able to leave.

Good answer: "We work on 3-month minimum terms. After that, 30 days notice. We'd rather keep you because we're delivering results, not because you're contractually obligated."

Bad answer: "Our standard contract is 12 months with a 90-day exit clause." (Why do they need 12 months? What are they afraid of you seeing at month 3?)

**Question 5: "Who owns the ad accounts? Are they in my name?"**

Why it matters: This is a critical ownership question. Some agencies run client campaigns from their own agency ad accounts. When you leave, you don't take the campaign history, the data, or sometimes even the audience lists. You start from zero. Your ad account history has real value — it affects Quality Scores, smart bidding performance, and audience data.

Good answer: "All campaigns run from ad accounts that belong to you. We have access as a partner. If you ever leave us, you keep the account, the data, everything."

Bad answer: "We manage everything from our agency account for efficiency." (Translation: we own your data and you'll lose it if you leave us.)

**Question 6: "What is your process for the first 30 days? What specifically will you do?"**

Why it matters: The first 30 days reveal everything about how an agency operates. A well-organised agency has a clear onboarding process. An agency winging it doesn't.

Good answer: "Week 1 is audit and strategy — we review your current setup, identify tracking gaps, and finalise campaign structure. Week 2 is build — we create landing pages, write ads, install tracking. Week 3 is launch — initial campaigns live with manual monitoring. Week 4 is first optimisation — we make adjustments based on initial data and report back."

Bad answer: "We'll get your campaigns live within the first week and start generating leads right away." (No mention of tracking. No mention of landing pages. Rushing to launch is a sign of inexperience.)

**Question 7: "How do you handle the period when campaigns aren't performing well? What's your escalation process?"**

Why it matters: Every campaign goes through rough patches. What separates great agencies from bad ones is not that things never go wrong — it's how they respond when they do.

Good answer: "If we don't hit agreed KPI benchmarks within 60 days, we have a structured review process: new creative testing, landing page audit, audience expansion. We proactively communicate before you have to ask. If we can't diagnose and fix the problem, we discuss extending the retainer period at no charge."

Bad answer: "Paid advertising takes time, results aren't always immediate." (This is the agency equivalent of "the cheque is in the post." It's a deflection, not a process.)

**Question 8: "Do you manage the landing pages and conversion rate optimisation, or just the ads?"**

Why it matters: An agency that only manages the ads and sends traffic to a bad landing page is like hiring a great chef to cook with bad ingredients. The ad gets you the click. The landing page gets you the lead. They must work together.

Good answer: "We either build and own the landing pages for you, or we work closely with your web team to ensure page quality matches our ad messaging. We treat CPL as the joint responsibility of ads AND landing pages."

Bad answer: "We focus on the ads — website is your responsibility." (This creates a gap in accountability. When results are bad, the agency blames the website. The website team blames the ads. You're caught in the middle.)

**Question 9: "Can you give me a reference — an existing client I can speak to?"**

Why it matters: Any agency with happy clients should be able to provide a reference. Reluctance to do so is a red flag. Even if the reference is curated (of course they'll give you their best client), the conversation itself is valuable — you learn how the agency communicates, how proactive they are, how they handle problems.

Good answer: "Yes, I'll connect you with two clients — one in a similar industry to yours. Feel free to ask them anything."

Bad answer: "Our clients prefer confidentiality due to competitive reasons." (This is occasionally legitimate. If every single client wants confidentiality, that's suspicious.)

**Question 10: "What happens to my campaigns and data if I decide to leave?"**

Why it matters: This is the question that reveals whether the agency has skin in the game or golden handcuffs strategy. The answer tells you how they think about the relationship.

Good answer: "You own everything. We'll do a proper handover — export all data, document campaign structure, brief your new provider or your in-house team. We want you to succeed even if you stop working with us."

Bad answer: Hesitation, vague answers, talk of "proprietary campaign structure" that they'll need to keep. This is a warning sign that the agency knows their value is in lock-in, not results.

---

### The One Question That Reveals Skin in the Game

If you can only ask one question: **"Do you offer any results guarantee?"**

An agency that is confident in their work has no problem guaranteeing it. They'll define what the guarantee covers, how it's measured, and what happens if they miss it.

An agency that refuses to guarantee anything is telling you they don't expect to deliver results consistently. They want your retainer regardless of outcome.

Guarantees should be specific: "We guarantee X leads per month by month 3 or we refund Y amount." Not vague: "We're committed to achieving great results for your business."

---

### Red Flags vs Yellow Flags

**Fire Them Immediately If:**
- They own your ad accounts and refuse to transfer them to you
- They cannot provide any specific campaign results with numbers
- They've been running your campaigns for 3+ months and cannot tell you your CPL
- You discover they've been spending budget on traffic sources they didn't disclose
- You find vanity metric reports (reach, impressions) masking the absence of lead data
- They increase your ad spend without consulting you and keep a percentage of spend as their fee

**Investigate Further If:**
- Monthly reports are always delivered late (disorganised, not necessarily dishonest)
- They're newer but can show you real work from previous roles or clients
- Their communication is inconsistent but their results data looks genuine
- Contract terms are longer than you'd like but everything else checks out

## SECTION 6: THE FULL CASE STUDY

*A cleaning company. No website. No ads. £200/month budget. Here's what happened.*

---

### The Brief

A domestic cleaning company in Salford, Greater Manchester. Operated by one founder with two part-time cleaners. No website. No ad history. No existing marketing budget to speak of.

The owner had been relying entirely on word-of-mouth and had plateaued at 12 regular clients. She needed 8-10 new clients per month to cover hiring a third full-time cleaner and make the business genuinely viable.

Her situation:
- Service area: 6-mile radius from central Salford
- Average client value: £120/month (4 cleans at £30 each)
- Average client retention: 7 months → lifetime value ~£840
- Available budget: £200/month for ads. We agreed to also build her a simple landing page.
- Target: 10+ qualified enquiries per month

---

### Every Decision Made — And Why

**Decision 1: Platform — Meta Only**

Using the decision framework from Section 1: this was a clear Meta Ads case.

Active demand: low. People don't typically Google "find me a new cleaner" unprompted. They hire cleaners through recommendations, local Facebook groups, or when something prompts the need (new house, baby, divorce, job change).

Ticket size: £120/month. A Google CPC of even £2.00 with a 5% conversion rate = £40 CPL. For a service worth £120/month that's viable but tight. With a Meta CPC of £0.60-£0.90, the economics become excellent.

Decision speed: medium. Cleaning is a considered purchase but not high-stakes. People act within days of seeing a compelling offer.

Budget: £200/month. Not enough to run meaningful Google Ads in a competitive city. Enough for a focused Meta Ads campaign with clear creative.

Verdict: Meta Ads, single platform, all budget concentrated.

**Decision 2: Creative — Video Over Static**

We shot a 28-second vertical video of the founder cleaning a kitchen. Real house, real mess, real transformation. The first 3 seconds: a cluttered, dirty kitchen. Cut to: sparkling surfaces, shot from the same angle. No words on screen. Just the transformation.

Audio: upbeat background track. Voiceover (her own voice): "I cleaned 47 homes in Salford last month. If yours is next, I'll give you your first clean at half price."

Why video over static image?

1. Shows the transformation viscerally — you can't communicate cleanliness as powerfully with a stock photo
2. The founder's own voice built immediate trust — this wasn't a faceless company
3. Video auto-plays in feed — it's a free pattern interrupt before anyone even chooses to engage
4. Video views are cheap on Meta — we built a warm audience of video viewers to retarget

**Decision 3: Targeting and CTA Mechanism**

Targeting: Women aged 28-55, Salford and 6-mile radius. Broad demographic, no interest layers. The video was doing the qualification — anyone who watches a 28-second cleaning video and doesn't find it relevant will scroll past naturally. No need to artificially narrow the audience.

CTA mechanism: WhatsApp. Not a form. Not a phone call. WhatsApp.

Why WhatsApp?

1. Lower friction than a form — one tap to start a conversation
2. Higher trust than a cold form submission — it's a personal channel
3. Converts as well or better for local consumer services than forms
4. Enables instant back-and-forth — the founder could answer "do you cover my postcode?" in real-time

The landing page was a single page: the transformation video, 8 bullet points about the service, 3 testimonials (from existing word-of-mouth clients she messaged to get testimonials from), and a single CTA button: "Message us on WhatsApp."

The WhatsApp link opened with a pre-filled message: "Hi, I saw your ad and I'm interested in a clean for my home. My address area is..."

Pre-filling removes the blank-page problem. People know what to say.

**Decision 4: Budget Structure**

£200 total. Single ad set. Single campaign. No splitting.

One temptation when managing a small budget is to test too many things at once: different creatives, different audiences, different CTAs. With £200/month (£6.50/day), splitting across multiple ad sets means £3.25/day each — too little for either to generate meaningful data.

We ran one ad set, one creative, and let it run for 14 days before any optimisation. The goal was for the algorithm to find its footing before we intervened.

---

### The Results

**Campaign period: 30 days**

- Total spend: £192 (slightly under £200 due to day 30 adjustment)
- Impressions: 41,200
- Link clicks to landing page: 610
- Landing page WhatsApp initiations: 47
- WhatsApp conversations that qualified as genuine enquiries: 27
- Bookings from those enquiries: 11
- Cost per landing page click: £0.31
- Cost per WhatsApp initiation: £4.09
- Cost per qualified enquiry: £7.25
- Cost per booked client: £17.45

The owner booked 11 new regular clients in 30 days from a £200 budget. At £120/month average client value and 7 months average retention: £9,240 in revenue generated from £192 in ad spend.

That is an 4,712% return on ad spend over the client lifetime. In month 1 alone, the 11 new clients generated £1,320 — a 587% return in month 1.

---

### The 3 Decisions That Drove the Result

**1. The right platform for the economics**

Had we used Google, the same £192 would have bought roughly 60-80 clicks at £2.40-£3.20 CPC. At the same 4.5% conversion rate to booking, we'd have generated 3 bookings. The economics of Meta for this service were 3-4x better than Google would have been.

**2. The video did the qualification**

A 28-second video of the actual service, by the actual founder, naturally filtered the audience. People who watched the whole thing were self-selecting as genuinely interested. By the time someone clicked to the landing page, they had already seen the quality of work and the face behind the business. Trust was partially built before they ever read a word.

**3. WhatsApp removed the friction at the critical moment**

We tested this hypothesis: local consumer services convert better on WhatsApp than on forms for audiences in this demographic. The hypothesis held. The pre-filled message removed the "what do I say?" hesitation. The founder responded to every message within 2 hours (we set a WhatsApp Business quick reply template). Conversion from conversation to booking was 41%.

### What This Means for Different Ticket Sizes

**Lower ticket (£50-£150 average): this model works, focus on volume**

The cleaning case study sits in this range. The key constraint is that you need high conversion rates to make CPL economics work. Video creative and WhatsApp/low-friction CTAs are essential. A form submission with an average response time of 24 hours will kill conversion rates at this ticket size.

**Mid-ticket (£300-£800 average): add a landing page with social proof**

A cosmetic dental consultation (£0 for the consult, leading to a £1,500-£3,000 treatment) or a building project consultation needs a higher-trust landing page. Add case studies, before/after photos, genuine reviews, and a strong guarantee. The CPL can be higher because the lifetime value justifies it.

**High-ticket (£1,000+ average): dual platform, retargeting essential**

Personal injury legal, major cosmetic procedures, B2B services. These buyers research extensively and rarely convert on first contact. Google captures the initial intent. Meta retargeting keeps you visible through the consideration period. Email sequences or a strong follow-up process converts the leads who went cold. The economics support a CPL of £100-£300+ in many cases.
